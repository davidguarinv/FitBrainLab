Animal Model Research

Brain Adaptations

Investigating neural mechanisms of stress resilience following voluntary exercise

Project Overview

This research project investigates the neurobiological mechanisms through which regular physical activity enhances resilience to stress. Using animal models, we are specifically focusing on the role of ΔFOSB and other molecular adaptations in key brain regions that occur following voluntary wheel running.

ΔFOSB is a transcription factor that accumulates in specific brain regions with repeated exposure to various stimuli, including exercise. Our hypothesis is that exercise-induced ΔFOSB accumulation in stress-regulatory brain circuits contributes to the stress-buffering effects of regular physical activity.

Research Methodology

Our research employs several advanced techniques:

  • Voluntary wheel running paradigms to model regular exercise in rodents
  • Immunohistochemistry and fluorescence microscopy to visualize ΔFOSB expression in brain tissue
  • Behavioral testing to assess stress resilience and anxiety-like behaviors
  • Viral-mediated gene transfer to manipulate ΔFOSB expression in specific brain regions

Current Progress

Our research has made significant progress in several areas:

  • Mapped the pattern of ΔFOSB accumulation in multiple brain regions following different durations of voluntary wheel running
  • Identified correlations between regional ΔFOSB expression and behavioral measures of stress resilience
  • Developed viral vectors to selectively manipulate ΔFOSB expression in specific neuronal populations

We are currently conducting experiments to determine the causal role of ΔFOSB in exercise-induced stress resilience by selectively increasing or decreasing its expression in key brain regions.

Key Findings

Our research has yielded several important findings:

  • Voluntary wheel running leads to significant ΔFOSB accumulation in the prefrontal cortex, hippocampus, and amygdala - key regions involved in stress regulation
  • The pattern of ΔFOSB expression differs between acute and chronic exercise, suggesting distinct neuroadaptations with regular physical activity
  • Animals with higher ΔFOSB expression in the prefrontal cortex show enhanced resilience to stress in behavioral tests

Translational Implications

This research has important implications for understanding how exercise benefits mental health in humans:

  • Identifying the molecular mechanisms of exercise-induced stress resilience may lead to novel therapeutic targets for stress-related disorders
  • Understanding the time course and dose-response relationship of these neuroadaptations can inform exercise prescription guidelines
  • The findings may help explain individual differences in the mental health benefits of exercise, potentially leading to personalized exercise recommendations
